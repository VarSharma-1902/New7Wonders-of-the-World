Chichen Itza
The Maya inhabitants of the Terminal Classic period created Chichen Itza, a vast pre-Columbian city. Tinm Municipality, Yucatán State, Mexico is home to the archaeological site.
From the Late Classic (c. AD 600–900) to the Terminal Classic (c. AD 800–900) and into the early Postclassic period (c. AD 900–1200), Chichen Itza was a key focal point in the Northern Maya Lowlands. The site features a variety of architectural styles that are reminiscent of central Mexican styles as well as the Puuc and Chenes styles of the Northern Maya lowlands.
Chichen Itza was one of the most important Maya towns, and it was most likely one of the legendary great cities, or Tollans, mentioned in later Mesoamerican literature.  The city may have had the most diversified population in the Maya civilization, which may have contributed to the site's numerous architectural styles.
About Chichen Itza

"Chichen Itza" is a Maya word that implies "At the mouth of the Itza's well,". This is derived from chi', which means "mouth" or "edge," and chen or cheen, which means "well." Itzá is the name of an ethnic-lineage group that ruled the northern peninsula in terms of politics and economics. From its (itz), "sorcerer," and ha, "water," one probable translation for Itza is "enchanter (or enchantment) of the water."
Prior to the emergence of the Itza hegemony in northern Yucatán, evidence in the Chilam Balam records suggests that this city had a different name. While most sources agree that the first word indicates seven, there is much disagreement about the rest of the translation. Because there is no single standard of orthography, this ancient name is variously written as Uuc Yabnal ("Seven Great House"), Uuc Hab Nal ("Seven Bushy Places"), Uucyabnal ("Seven Great Rulers"), or Uc Abnal ("Seven Great Rulers") ("Seven Lines of Abnal"). This Late Classic Period name appears in both the book of Chilam Balam de Chumayel and hieroglyphic inscriptions found in the ruins.
