About Petra

Petra, also known as Raqmu or Raqm by its locals, is a historic and archaeological city in southern Jordan. It is located next to the Jabal Al-Madbah mountain, in a basin surrounded by mountains that constitute the eastern flank of the Arabah valley, which runs from the Dead Sea to the Gulf of Aqaba. Petra has been inhabited since at least 7000 BC, and the Nabataeans may have resided in what would become their kingdom's capital city as early as the 4th century BC. The Nabataeans were nomadic Arabs who established Petra as a key regional economic hub because of its closeness to the incense trade routes.

Although the Nabataean kingdom became a client state of the Roman Empire in the first century BC, it was only in 106 AD that it lost its independence. Petra fell to the Romans, who annexed Nabataea and renamed it as Arabia Petraea. Petra's importance declined as sea trade routes emerged, and after an earthquake in 363 destroyed many structures. In the Byzantine era several Christian churches were built, but the city continued to decline, and by the early Islamic era it was abandoned except for a handful of nomads. It remained unknown until it was rediscovered in 1812 by Johann Ludwig Burckhardt.

Integrity
All of the major freestanding and rock-cut structures, as well as extensive archaeological remains within the arid landscape of red sandstone cliffs and gorges, are contained within the property's boundaries, which overlap with the Petra National Park's boundaries. Wind and rain continue to erode the monuments, which has been compounded in the past by windblown sand caused by grazing animals depleting ground cover. The relocation of the Bdul (Bedouin) tribe and their livestock from their former seasonal homes in the Petra basin to a new hamlet at Umm Sayhun more than two decades ago was intended to halt this process.
If the Nabataean diversion system is not constantly checked, repaired, and maintained, they are likewise subject to flash floods through Wadi Musa through the winding valley (Siq).
The site is also subject to local community and tourism infrastructure demands. Within the property to the north, a new wastewater treatment facility has been installed, with reclaimed water being used for an adjacent drip irrigation farming project. Inside the boundary, additional infrastructure development is proposed, including an electricity supply and substation, a community/visitor centre, an outdoor theatre for community events, picnic areas, a camping ground, and a new restaurant near the Qasr al Bint temple, all of which have the potential to compromise the property's integrity.
